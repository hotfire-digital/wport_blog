Before you start working in Taiwan, one thing matters even more than landing a job: **make sure you have legal work eligibility.**

Many overseas students assume an offer means they can start right away. In reality, working without a work permit can put both you and the employer at legal risk.

And starting in 2026, Taiwan introduced a new rule that is especially helpful for overseas students. Many people still do not know about it. This article walks through the process, the new system, and the key points to watch.

## 1. Who needs to apply for a work permit?

In Taiwan, these three groups of students must obtain a work permit before starting work:

- **Overseas Chinese students** (studying in Taiwan with overseas Chinese student status)
- **International students** (studying in Taiwan on a student visa or residence permit)
- **Hong Kong and Macau students** (studying in Taiwan with Hong Kong/Macau resident status)

Whether it is full-time work, a part-time job, or an internship, if there is pay, this rule applies.

---

## 2. 2026 new rule: a “permit-free” period after graduation

This is the most important policy change to watch in 2026.

Starting **January 1, 2026**, eligible overseas students may **work without applying for a work permit** for a limited period after graduation. Employers also do not need to file an extra application.

### Eligibility

You must meet all of the following:

- Completed studies at a Taiwan junior college or higher (associate degree or above)
- Obtained extended residence eligibility after graduation
- Status as an overseas Chinese student or Mainland Chinese student
- For those who graduated before 2026: must be within two years after graduation, and must never have held a work permit before

### Permit-free work period

| Status | Period |
|------|------|
| Overseas Chinese students, Mainland Chinese students | Up to 2 years (1 year + 1-year extension) |
| Hong Kong and Macau students | Up to 1 year (6 months + 6-month extension) |

### What are the benefits during this period?

- Employers **do not need** to apply for a separate work permit
- **No job-category restriction**. You do not necessarily have to take “specialized or technical work”
- Full-time, part-time, or project-based work are all possible
- The usual foreign-employee minimum salary threshold **does not apply** (you only need to meet the basic wage of NT$29,500)

### What happens after the permit-free period ends?

When the period expires, the employer must return to the original hiring rules, apply for a work permit again, and job-category limits and employer qualification thresholds apply once more.

💡 Residence permits issued from 2026 onward will note “work permit not required” and the expiry date directly on the card, making it easier for employers to verify.

## 3. Standard work permit process: 4 steps

If you are not in the permit-free period, or the period has ended, follow the formal process:

### Step 1: Find an employer willing to hire you

You do not apply for the work permit yourself. The **employer applies to the Ministry of Labor on your behalf**.

The first step is finding a company that has offered you the role and is willing to help with the work permit. That is why foreigner-friendly employers matter so much. Not every company has the willingness or experience.

### Step 2: The employer files online

The employer prepares your residence permit, passport, employment contract, and other documents, then submits the application online through the Ministry of Labor’s foreign professional work permit system.

You do not need to run this part yourself, but you should actively check progress with the employer instead of waiting silently.

### Step 3: Wait for review (about 7 working days)

When documents are complete, review usually takes **7 working days from the day after receipt**. If more documents are needed, it will take longer.

Start the application at least three weeks before your planned start date so both sides have buffer time.

### Step 4: Update your residence permit after approval

After the work permit is approved, bring the permit to the National Immigration Agency to update your Alien Resident Certificate (ARC) and formally register your work eligibility. Only then is the process complete.

---

## 4. What documents should you prepare before applying?

These are the documents you need to give the employer. Make sure they are still valid:

- Passport (with enough remaining validity)
- Alien Resident Certificate (ARC)

The employer also needs to prepare:

- Business certificate and the filer’s Citizen Digital Certificate
- Review fee receipt
- Application form and list of employed foreigners
- Copy of the employment contract (must include employer name, your name, job title, period, and salary)
- Copy of the company responsible person’s ID or passport
- Copy of company registration certificate

## 5. Important reminders

### ⚠️ Do not work before the work permit is approved

Getting an offer and signing a contract does not mean you can start immediately. You may legally begin only **after the work permit is approved**. (Except during the permit-free period.)

### ⚠️ Permit validity usually matches your residence permit

Work permit validity is usually tied to the residence permit. When your ARC is about to expire, extend the work permit at the same time so you do not fall into a gap.

### ⚠️ Changing jobs requires a new application

A work permit is issued for a specific employer. Changing companies means a new permit. The new employer must apply again. The old one cannot be reused.

## 6. FAQ

**Q: Can a work permit application be rejected?**

If documents are complete and the employer qualifies, rejection is uncommon. But if the employer has violation records, or your residence status does not meet the requirements, the case may be returned.

**Q: Do part-time jobs also need a work permit?**

Yes. As long as there is pay, any form of work needs a work permit (or must fall within the permit-free period).

**Q: Do I qualify for the 2026 permit-free rule?**

It mainly depends on two things: whether you graduated from a Taiwan junior college or higher, and whether you obtained extended residence eligibility after graduation. If you are unsure, check your situation with WPORT.
