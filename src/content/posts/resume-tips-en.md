You have already sent several resumes, but interview invitations never arrive?

The problem may not be your ability. It may be that your resume “makes Taiwan employers unable to understand you.”

Taiwan Chinese resumes follow a set of conventions that differ from habits in other countries. This article covers five resume details Taiwan employers care about most, so you can check quickly and spot the issues.

## Tip 1: A photo is required, and it has to be the right photo

Taiwan resumes almost always include a photo, unlike practices in many Western countries. But “having a photo” is not enough. Photo quality directly shapes an employer’s first impression.

According to data, resumes with an appropriate photo are **3 times** more likely to get interview invitations than those without one.

What counts as an acceptable photo?

- Simple, clean background; solid or white works best. Avoid cluttered settings
- Neat, professional clothing; solid tops or business casual are both fine
- Natural expression, front-facing shot, eyes looking at the camera
- Clear image quality and good lighting. Do not use selfies or travel photos

Photos are usually placed in the top-right or top-left corner. Not too large, not too small. Roughly the proportion of a standard ID photo.

## Tip 2: Clearly state nationality and visa status in your basic info

This is the item overseas students most often miss, but Taiwan employers care about it a lot.

Before deciding whether to interview you, employers usually want to confirm one thing first: “Can this person work legally in Taiwan?”

If your resume does not explain this clearly, employers may skip you rather than ask more questions.

In the basic information section, clearly list:

- **Nationality**
- **Residence permit status** (for example: holding a valid Alien Resident Certificate, ARC)
- **Work permit status** (for example: employer can apply on your behalf, or you already hold a work permit)
- **LinkedIn profile** (add it if you have one, so employers can quickly review your full background)

Also consider adding a **one-minute self-introduction** of 30 to 50 words that highlights your core strengths, for example: “Internship experience, strong multilingual communication, and the ability to adapt quickly in cross-cultural workplaces.”

## Tip 3: Quantify work experience. Do not only say “responsible for XX”

This is the most common resume issue. Not only overseas students. Many local Taiwanese applicants make the same mistake.

**❌ Bad example:**
> “Responsible for social media management”

**✅ Good example:**
> “Managed 3 brand social platforms; grew followers 40% in 6 months; improved average post reach by 25%”

For every job or internship, try to answer one question: “What did you do, and what results did you achieve?”

Resumes with numbers help employers feel your real contribution.

If you have cross-border work or study experience, highlight it. Cross-cultural communication and overseas background are pluses for many Taiwan companies. Do not hide them.

If you just graduated and have no full-time experience, that is fine. Include internships, school projects, clubs, volunteering, and competitions. The key is still using numbers to show what you did.

## Tip 4: Attach specific certificate levels for language ability

Taiwan employers take language skills seriously. Your multilingual edge needs concrete proof. Writing “Fluent English” alone is not enough.

According to data, foreign applicants who include Chinese language certificates on their resumes are **1.4 times** more likely to get interview invitations.

List them like this:

- Chinese: Native / Fluent (if not native, include TOCFL level)
- English: TOEIC XXX / IELTS X.X / TOEFL XXX
- Japanese: JLPT N2 / N1
- Other languages: state your actual level, and add certificates when you have them

Let employers judge your language level at a glance, without guessing.

## Tip 5: Structure your autobiography and explain “why Taiwan”

Taiwan Chinese resumes usually include an autobiography. Aim for **300 to 500 characters**.

An autobiography is not a list of degrees. It needs a clear structure:

1. **Brief self-introduction**: education background and core strengths
2. **Why you chose Taiwan**: real, specific reasons that show sincerity and planning
3. **Career direction**: what you hope to grow into in this role, and where you are headed next

“Why do you want to work in Taiwan?” comes up in almost every interview. Giving a persuasive answer in your autobiography helps employers form a strong impression while reading your resume.

## Quick summary of the 5 tips

| Item | Key points |
|------|----------|
| Photo | Solid background, front-facing, clear and professional; having a photo raises interview odds by 3x |
| Basic info | State nationality, residence permit, and work permit status so employers do not have to guess |
| Work experience | Quantify results, highlight cross-cultural background, let numbers speak |
| Language ability | Add specific certificates and scores; Chinese certificates raise odds by 1.4x |
| Autobiography | 300 to 500 characters; explain why Taiwan and your career plan |

---

## One last reminder: write your resume in Traditional Chinese

This may sound obvious, but some applicants still send Simplified Chinese or English resumes for Taiwan roles.

Unless the job specifically asks for an English resume, always write in **Traditional Chinese**. It is basic respect for Taiwan employers, and it helps you avoid being filtered out at the first stage.
