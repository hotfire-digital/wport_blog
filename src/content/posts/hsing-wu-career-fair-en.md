In early June, the Wport team visited **Hsing Wu University of Science and Technology’s career fair**. The energy on site was strong, especially among overseas students who came to our booth. You could see the hope of “building a future in Taiwan” in their eyes.

As a job platform focused on matching overseas students with startups, we turned the booth into a “**pop-up resume clinic**.” After reviewing dozens of resumes, we found most people were stuck on the same four blind spots. That is a shame, because each one has a clear fix.

This piece focuses on a few resume-prep directions, with **rewrite formulas you can copy directly**.

## Blind spot 1: Chronological laundry-list resumes. HR wants “concrete output” and “quantified results”

When students write about restaurant front-desk part-time work, it often looks like this: “XX Restaurant, part-time staff, 2025/03–2026/01,” and then nothing else.

But what HR is thinking is: **“I know you worked at a restaurant. What exactly did you do? What skills did you show? What value does that bring to our company?”**

### Wport rewrite formula

**Formula:** Specific duties + quantified numbers (headcount / times / items / percentages) + recognition of results

**Before (laundry list)**

> XX Restaurant, part-time staff, 2025/03–2026/01

**After (quantified results)**

> Collaborated efficiently with a cross-border team of **20 people**; during peak hours set a record of serving **40 groups in one hour**; memorized ingredients and features of **45 dishes**, and was selected **Employee of the Month twice** for strong performance.

With numbers (20 people, 40 groups, 45 dishes, 2 times), the difference is clear. HR reviews a huge volume of resumes every day, and many now also use AI to screen them. How you present numbers is the key.

## Blind spot 2: No customization. Part-time experience can be reframed across fields

One student asked anxiously on site: “I want to join a startup marketing team later. Does that mean my past restaurant part-time experience is completely useless?”

Do not think that way. **Every job has transferable value. The key is the angle you use.**

For the same restaurant part-time experience, if you are applying specifically to a “**marketing company**,” we would reframe it like this:

> Handled frontline customer relationship management (CRM), demonstrated crisis handling and communication during peak hours; successfully **raised new-item order rate by 15% in one month** through verbal recommendations, showing consumer insight close to market demand.

See the difference? Same restaurant job. With a marketing lens, you move from “part-timer who carried plates” to “**a marketing talent who understands frontline consumer psychology**.”

> Quick tip: One resume does not fit every role. Target the job you want, and retranslate your experience into the other side’s language. Interview invitation rates will look very different.

## Blind spot 3: Do not underestimate club experience. It is your career incubator

Many students feel that “without a company internship, the resume is blank.” That is wrong. **Club experience absolutely counts.**

- Raised sponsorships in the department student association → you understand Business Development
- Organized a hundred-person recital in the guitar club → you understand Project Management
- Served as chair of the overseas-student association → you understand cross-cultural leadership and resource coordination

These are all assets. Just apply Blind Spot 1’s quantification rule: **how much sponsorship you raised, how many people attended the event, how large a team you led.** Done right, club work can shine on a resume too.

## Blind spot 4: Starting too late. Sophomore year is the golden positioning window

Job hunting does not begin in senior year after graduation. Especially for overseas students who want to stay in Taiwan, you need time to accumulate **point-based evaluation** scores (language tests, internships, licenses, and more). None of that can be patched together overnight.

> How is the point system calculated, and what combinations can get you to 70 points? We break it down further here: [2026 Point System Changes for Staying in Taiwan: How Overseas Students Should Plan Early](/blog/posts/2026-point-system-taiwan-stay-changes).

We strongly recommend this: **start building your resume file in sophomore year.** From now on, intentionally accumulate part-time work with real output, take certifications, and join clubs. While others scramble after senior-year graduation, you will already have a polished data-driven resume and room to choose the stay-in-Taiwan roles you want. In the point system, one year or half a year of work or internship experience also adds points. If you wait until senior year to prepare, it will already be too late.

## One-table recap: 4 blind spots × matching fixes

| Blind spot | What HR wonders | Wport fix |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Laundry-list resume | What did you actually do? | Quantify concrete output with data |
| 2. No customization | How does this relate to our role? | Reframe through the target job’s lens |
| 3. Undervaluing clubs | Do you have hands-on experience? | Write clubs as project management / business development |
| 4. Preparing too late | Are your stay-in-Taiwan points enough? | From sophomore year, accumulate verifiable point-system assets |

## Closing: Turn anxiety into a stay-in-Taiwan progress bar

The Hsing Wu University fair reminded us of one thing again: overseas students do not lack experience. What they lack is **translating experience into language employers understand**. The four blind spots look scattered, but the core is one sentence: **make your value visible and verifiable.**
