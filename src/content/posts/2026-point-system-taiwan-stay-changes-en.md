Many overseas and international graduates want to stay in Taiwan after graduation, but get stuck on salary thresholds or employers who are reluctant to hire. Taiwan has promoted the **point system** since 2014 (ROC Year 103) so graduates do not have to rely on high salary alone. Education, language, internships, and other factors can be combined to meet the bar.

2026 brings another wave of practical changes. This article puts policy highlights, score strategy, and a timeline in one place so you can plan early.

## 1. 2026 key update: job seeking within two years after graduation may not require a work permit first

From ROC Years 103 to 113, the number of overseas and international students grew from 40,079 to 78,801. The point system did raise stay-and-work opportunities, but only about 17,530 obtained work visas in the same period. Growth happened, yet the conversion rate remains low.

The bottleneck is often on the employer side: hiring foreign talent is still constrained by revenue, industry category, and other conditions, and paperwork plus timelines scare many companies away. To support the National Development Council's goal of retaining 190,000 overseas and international graduates by 2030, the Executive Yuan NDC introduced a new 2026 policy: **during a two-year post-graduation job-seeking period, you may work without a work visa first**. In that window, companies are less bound by existing hiring conditions, so foreign graduates become noticeably more hireable.

---

## 2. Time without a work permit usually does not count toward APRC

Working without a work permit has trade-offs. If your long-term goal is **permanent residence (APRC)**, note carefully: periods working in Taiwan without a work visa **usually cannot be counted toward APRC qualifying years**. Treat this stage as a bridge to "build experience, then land the next job with a formal work permit," not as a permanent plan.

---

## 3. Stay-and-work reality: stay first, then move up

Under this policy, a pragmatic path looks like this:

1. **Prioritize roles that build your resume** and help you stay in Taiwan. Within the two-year job-seeking period, you can switch jobs more freely.
2. **Use industry trends**: With AI momentum, startups need more PM, product, and cross-border talent (especially Southeast Asia). Barriers are sometimes lower than at large companies.
3. **Treat the first job as leverage**: The hardest part is often the first offer. Its purpose is not a "perfect destination," but a bridge to a better second role. By then, asking an employer to help with a work permit is often more realistic.

---

## 4. Employer "processability" becomes decisive

Even if your score is enough, if the employer does not know the process or fears long delays, they may simply not hire foreign graduates.
So job hunting is no longer only resume competition. It is also whether you can make the company feel safe handling the paperwork.

Recommended approach:

- Proactively explain in interviews that you qualify under the point system
- Prepare a one-page process brief (steps, timeline, documents)
- Make HR feel that "this is controllable"

## 5. Score sources shift from "single strength" to "combination"

Many people used to bet on one strong item (for example education or language alone). A more stable approach now is **combinational**:

- Degree + language + internship
- Certificates + projects + Taiwan work experience
- Salary conditions + professional competencies

The advantage of combinations: if one item changes, you still have a buffer. During school, audit your score regularly. If you cannot even reach 70 points, job hunting and staying will be tough.

A few common "baseline" references (always verify against current regulations and your documents):

| Item | Approx. points |
|------|----------|
| Bachelor's degree | 10 points |
| Hiring salary at the minimum tier (e.g. annual salary NT$315,020) | 10 points |
| Internship of at least one year during studies | 10 points |
| Chinese proficiency at the minimum level | 20 points |
| Native language (other-country language ability) | 10 points |
| Second foreign-language certificate (e.g. English, Japanese) | +10 more points |

Those items alone can approach 60 points. Add other bonuses and **reaching 70 in Taiwan is not hard**. The key is that every item must have **verifiable documents**. Especially for a second foreign-language certificate: if you have the certificate, you get the points, regardless of your other scores.

## 6. Action rhythm from 12 months before graduation to month 0

If you are still in school, this stretch matters most. Here is a timeline you can execute directly.

### T-12 to T-9 (months)

- Inventory the score items you can claim now
- Identify the two highest-gap dimensions (usually language and experience)
- Decide which certificate, internship, and portfolio to fill

This stage is not about sending many resumes. It is about building verifiable assets first.

### T-8 to T-4 (months)

- Start applying to foreigner-friendly companies in earnest
- Accumulate interview Q&A and common employer concerns
- Prepare application document templates in parallel (Chinese/English resumes, degrees, proofs)

In this period, make your "job-hunting materials" and "application materials" convertible into each other.

### T-3 to T (months)

- Lock employers and roles that can process the point system
- Confirm whether offer terms and application timing can actually land
- Build a backup plan (second job option, short bridging options)

In the final three months before graduation, stop broad exploration. Converge on an executable path.

---

## 7. The three most common 2026 mistakes

### Mistake 1: Scores are "estimated," not "calculated"

Many people judge by impression that they "should be enough."
But at real application time, one missing document can make an item unscored.

**Fix:** Every point must map to a document. No document means no points.

### Mistake 2: Telling the employer about the process too late

If HR only discovers foreign hiring paperwork at the end, the common result is delay or even a cancelled offer.

**Fix:** Communicate the legal path and timeline before the second interview round.

### Mistake 3: Separating the point system from career planning

You are not applying for the sake of applying. You want a long-term career in Taiwan.
If the role is a mismatch, even approval can stall quickly.

**Fix:** Choose the right industry and function first, then design your score strategy.

---

## 8. A practical 2026 strategy for overseas and international graduates

If you remember only three things, remember these:

1. **Scores must be provable**: every item has formal documents.
2. **Employers must be persuadable**: show that process and risk are controllable.
3. **Paths need backups**: do not put staying in Taiwan on a single offer or a single score item.

The point system was never a last-minute rescue. It is a stay-and-work project you should start designing while still in school.

---

## Closing

The 2026 direction makes it easier to start staying in Taiwan within two years after graduation. Long term, though, you still need a complete plan around work permits, scores, and career.

From university onward, deliberately practice Chinese, earn a second foreign language, and accumulate internships. After graduation, use the job-seeking period to build real experience, then connect to a formal work permit and the APRC path. Once you execute this checklist, the point system stops being pressure and becomes a path you can control.
